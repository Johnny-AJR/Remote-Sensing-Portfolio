Conceptual Foundation

Six expandable field notes from first principles to common mistakes.

Remote sensing records reflected or emitted energy from the surface. A satellite sensor such as Sentinel-2 MSI samples that energy in narrow wavelength bands, letting us compare how crops, water, soil, ash, and minerals behave without walking every field.

For crop work, the strongest signal usually comes from the contrast between visible red absorption by chlorophyll and near-infrared scattering by leaf structure.

Spectral indices combine bands to reduce illumination differences and amplify a target process. Normalized differences such as NDVI and NDWI keep values in a stable -1 to +1 range, while ratio and composite indices can be unbounded and must be interpreted locally.

  • Difference: highlights contrast between two materials.
  • Ratio: reduces overall brightness effects.
  • Soil-adjusted form: reduces exposed-soil influence in sparse vegetation.

Key insight: NDVI exploits the red absorption trough and the NIR plateau. Healthy leaves absorb red light for photosynthesis and scatter NIR through internal leaf structure, so a high NIR-minus-red contrast usually means active green biomass.

  • Cloud, haze, smoke, and shadows can dominate the signal; mask them before interpretation.
  • Constants in EVI2, SAVI, OSAVI, and WI2015 expect reflectance scaled to 0-1.
  • Mixed pixels matter near field boundaries, canals, roads, and orchards with exposed soil.
  • Thresholds shift with crop variety, irrigation, soil background, phenological stage, and atmosphere.
  • Single-date classification is fragile; time-series context is usually stronger for crop separation.

Use NDVI or EVI2 for general crop vigor, NDRE for chlorophyll in denser canopies, NDMI/MSI for water stress, BSI/SAVI when soil background is visible, NBR/dNBR for burn severity, and MNDWI/AWEI for water extraction.

For field decisions, compare the same crop at the same growth stage and prefer stable anomalies over one-off absolute thresholds.
